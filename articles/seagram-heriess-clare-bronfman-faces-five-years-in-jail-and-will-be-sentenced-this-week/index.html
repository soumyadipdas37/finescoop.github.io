Reading now: Seagram heriess Clare Bronfman faces five years in jail and will be sentenced this week

Seagrams liquor heiress Clare Bronfman will be sentenced this week and prosecutors are demanding she serve five years in prison as she refuses to denounce her loyalty to sex cult Nxivm and its notorious leader Keith Raniere.

Bronfman, 41, will be the first defendant to be sentenced in the lengthy Nxivm case on Wednesday.

She faces up to five years in prison, as suggested by the United States attorney’s office in Brooklyn on September 14, alleging the group wouldn't have been so powerful without her support. 

Her lawyers argue she should not go to prison at all because believed the group was a force for good.

NXIVM started out as a purported self-help group in Albany, New York, before members were turned into sex slaves, made to live off just 500 calories a day, and branded with Raniere's initials, and were groomed to have sex with him. 

The hearing – the first major one to unfold in the federal courthouse in Brooklyn since the pandemic began – will be the first time victims and former members of Nxivm will speak out to the judge by video and in-person statements.

Bronfman is believed to have used her family’s fortune to bankroll the cult and in April 2019 she pleaded guilty to two crimes in connection to the alleged cult – conspiring to conceal and harbor illegal immigrants for financial gain and fraudulent use of identification. 

'Many people, including most of my own family, believe I should disavow Keith and Nxivm, and that I have not is hard for them to understand and accept,' Bronfman wrote in a letter on August 28 to the judge. 

'However, for me, Nxivm and Keith greatly changed my life for the better,' she added. 

Nxivm fell into the national spotlight for its famous members including actess Nicki Clyne of 'Battlestar Gallactica'; actress Allison Mack of TV's 'Smallville'; India Oxenberg, daughter of TV star Catherine Oxenberg of 'Dynasty' fame; and physician Danielle Roberts. 

Bronfman was one of the most devout members of the group. 

Bronfman joined Nxivm in 2003 after hearing about the group from her sister and father Edgar Bronfman, the former chairman of the Seagram Company. 

She quit her equestrian career to move to Nxivm’s headquarters in upstate New York and funded it by giving millions to the cause.

According to letters written by her friends to the court she was always shy and lacking in self-confidence and her insecurity drove her to seek a sense of purpose within Nxivm. 

Bronfman used her wealth to hire lawyers, private investigators and public relations firms to discredit critics of Nxivm.

In total Bronfman spent more than $100million on her inherited wealth into funding Nxivm’s legal battles. She also gave Raniere $67million to invest in commodities market, which he never repaid, prosecutors said. 

In the case six of the shadowy organization’s leaders and prominent members will be criminally charged. 

In the case Bronfman and four others pleaded guilty.

Raniere co-founded the program in 1998 and since then around 18,000 people have taken its self-help courses, which cost thousands of dollars each.  

In 2015, he then formed Dominus Obsequious Sororium (DOS) - a secret master/slave club where women were branded, turned into his sex slaves, given restricted diets and forced to make a lifetime vow to obey their 'masters'. 

The cult is also accused of forcing victims to undergo illegal human experiments.   

A lawsuit filed in January also claims the self-improvement group was a pyramid scheme that swindled millions of dollars out of its victims based on false scientific claims such as that it could cure medical conditions like Tourette's syndrome and OCD.

At least 40 members of the cult have also claimed they were part of a 'human fright experiment' in which electrodes were used on their heads to measure brainwaves, according to the lawsuit. 

The members believed they were going to be watching Raniere speak but were actually shown graphic footage or five women being beheaded and dismembered in Mexico.

Many who participated in the courses were left traumatized and broke, according to the lawsuit.    

Actresses Nicki Clyne (left ) and Allison Mack were also members of the cult

Bronfman has denied any involvement in the sex slave arm of the cult saying she was 'never told about anything sexual or damaging of any nature'.

Other members have also written to the judge insisting she was not involved in DOS.  

Raniere was arrested at a Mexican hideout in 2018 following an investigation into the group. 

Raniere was the sole defendant to go to trial, resulting in his June 2019 conviction for racketeering, sex trafficking, and other crimes. 

Raniere will be sentenced on October 27. Prosecutors have asked the judge to sentence him to life in prison.

In a submission to the court this month, Raniere's lawyers said he had no remorse and stands by his innocence. He has accused the government of misconduct and says he deserves a new trial.  

Throughout the pandemic several cult followers have danced outside the Metropolitan Detention Center in Brooklyn where he is being held in a show of support for their leader and 'master'. 

The sentencings for the other defendants have not yet been scheduled.
