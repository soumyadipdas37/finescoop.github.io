Reading now: Morrisons boss Covid triggered the renaissance of supermarkets

Supermarket Morrisons has taken a £155million hit to its profits as a result of the coronavirus pandemic, fresh figures show.

The Bradford-based grocer saw its pre-tax profits drop just over 25 per cent to £148million in the six months to 2 August.

But, with shoppers scrambling to get hold of essential supplies at the start of lockdown, like-for-like sales rose nearly 9 per cent over the period.

To help cope with increased demand, the supermarket initially hired 45,000 extra staff, leaving boss David Potts claiming the pandemic triggered a 'renaissance' of Britain's supermarkets.

Higher sales: Morrisons saw sales rise nearly 9% in the last six months 

Morrisons said its online and home delivery order capacity rose five-fold since lockdown started to help meet demand, with five new growth channels, namely, Morrisons.com store pick, food boxes, doorstep, Morrisons on Amazon and Deliveroo, now operating. 

'The pandemic has really speed-up the use of technology in the sector, and Morrisons are keeping up with the trend', David Madden, an analyst at CMC Markets UK, said. 

Amid an unrelenting supermarket price war, Morrisons announced this week that it planned to cut the price of 400 essential products across its stores with immediate effect.

Recent research by consumer group Which? placed Morrisons in fifth place in a table ranking the total cost of a 74-item shop.  

David Potts, chief executive of Morrisons, said today: 'From the start of the pandemic we stepped up and put the company's assets at the disposal of the country to help feed the nation.

'Morrisons is at the heart of local communities and responded quickly when it mattered most, and we are very grateful for the British public's appreciation of all the vital work our colleagues are doing.

'I believe we are seeing the renaissance of British supermarkets.

'We are now looking forward to holding on to what we created in the first half, building on our colleagues' inspiration and innovation, and sustaining the momentum of a broader, stronger Morrisons.'

The supermarket said it incurred 'many extra costs' at the peak of the pandemic, including staff hires, kitting out stores to make them compliant with Government rules and protective clothing for staff.

While extra costs affected its bottom line, Morrisons said the net hit came in at £62million because of business rates relief of £93million. 

Total revenues fell 1.1 per cent to £8.73billion over the period, reflecting the loss of fuel sales as people stayed at home.

He's in charge: The boss of Morrisons, David Potts

On the up: Morrisons has upped its online operations and home delivery capacity 

Morrisons said it planned to reward staff with a guaranteed annual bonus of 6 per cent this year.

It hiked its interim dividend by 5.7 per cent to 2.04p a share and forecasts continued sales momentum in the second half of the year, particularly with fuel sales set to increase. The group said a decision about a special dividend for shareholders remained 'deferred.'

FTSE 100-listed Morrisons saw its share price drop this morning. Shares in the supermarket are currently down 4.38 per cent or 8.55p to 186.45p. 

David Madden, an analyst at CMC Markets UK, said: 'Morrisons share price saw a jump in volatility in March, at the start of the lockdown. 

'It initially sold-off as the wider sentiment in the markets was dreadful, but then there was a rebound as the group was allowed to remain open amid the pandemic as it is an essential service. 

'There was a surge in demand for food items has the public stocked-up on goods. Traders cottoned-on that supermarkets would actually benefit from the health emergency and that's why Morrisons share price has been pushing higher in the last few months.'

He added: 'Morrisons share price has been pushing higher since March, and if the broader bullish run continues, it might target the 210 zone.' 

Meanwhile, Connor Campbell, an analyst at Spreadex, said: 'The reaction to Morrison's update contributed to the FTSE's problems, with the supermarket stock slumping by 4.5%. 

'This also sent a ripple of concern down the aisles of its peers, with Sainsbury's slipping 2% and Tesco falling 1.4%. Even their online cousin wasn't immune from a bit of anxiety, with Ocado dipping 1.1%.'
